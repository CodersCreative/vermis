When designing the project we knew that there were a couple major hurdles that would determine its effectiveness,
usability and customer base in Zimbabwe and Southern Africa as a whole.
We decided after consulting farmers across the country that these factors were:

import { Card, CardGrid, LinkButton } from '@astrojs/starlight/components';

<CardGrid stagger>
  <Card title="Price">
    The average Zimbabwean only makes around $2400 per year paired with the fact that this project’s value greatly
    increases with the effect that these pests have had on a farmer further lowering their income made it obvious to us
    that making it as cheap as possible would be the best route forward.
    Primarily to help reduce the cost of the rover we prioritized the use of locally available materials and low-cost microcontrollers
    to ensure the final unit cost remains accessible to small-scale communal farmers.  
  </Card>
  <Card title="Waterproofing">
    Nationally Zimbabwe records around 500 to 750 mm of rainfall annually and with some pests such as army worm striving and
    spreading faster during heavy rain we decided that allowing the rover to work in this weather
    would help further stop the spread of pests across farm lands within the country.
    Furthermore waterproofing removes the chance of the device being destroyed if not properly stored when its raining
    further reducing the amount of stress and worries these farmers experience  
  </Card>
  <Card title="Night time capabilities">
    By allowing the rover to work during night time we can allow farmers with larger pieces of land to require less rovers
    to keep adequate coverage of their crops as the device can be working through the day and night.
    To help the rover work at night we equipped it with a range of low-cost energy-efficient LEDs
    that can adequately light up its surroundings for the use of the integrated camera.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Autonomously controlled">
    One of our mai goals was to reduce the amount of people dedicated to combatting pesticides so by making it autonomously controlled
    we reduce the need for personnel to be hired to control and manage the rovers effectively.
    Furthermore it reduces the barrier to entry as the setup process greatly simplifies and farmers who wouldn’t have received lots of training
    in control devices such as these can now feel comfortable knowing that device will work to the best of its abilities
    without their assistance or the need for specialized expertise.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

So with these requirements in hand we set out designing and building a scale model of the final device.
This model would still be fully functional but primarily only work with smaller crops as it would not be able to sufficiently observe larger crops such as maize.
The model still remains with the ability to fully autonomously manage pesticide dispatch on farm land
and would later be used for our experiments to showcase the effectiveness of this design.
